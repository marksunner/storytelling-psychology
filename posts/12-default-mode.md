# Default-Mode and Social Insight

*By Mark Sunner — Digital Ape Training*
*October 10, 2023*

---

Have you ever had an idea pop into your head unexpectedly, only to later realise it was the perfect solution to a problem you were facing? As it turns out, the human brain is wired in a way that makes these "out of the blue" insights deliberately possible. Recent discoveries in neuroscience have clarified the purpose of "the default network," a neural configuration that the brain falls into when we're *not* actively engaged in any form of task or active thought process.

According to research by neuroscientist Dr. Matthew Lieberman, the default network is almost identical to the one used for social thinking, or "making sense of other people and ourselves." This means that whenever our brain has an idle/free moment, it automatically reflexes to think about other people. Specifically, *their* thoughts, aspirations and motives — and how this information may be of social benefit, competition or harm to ourselves.

---

## Why Does the Brain Default to Social Thinking?

But why would the brain, which only makes up 2 percent of our body weight but consumes over 20 percent of its energy, use its limited resources on 'social thinking' instead of relaxing & conserving energy? The answer, is that evolution has made a "bet" that the best thing for our brain to do in any spare moment is to get ready for what comes next in 'social' terms.

What is social thinking specifically, you may ask? Our hereditary programming has learned that the benefits of strong social bonds are numerous — we are pack animals. It is speculated that our brain might be using this time to integrate new experiences into our long-standing knowledge of other people's minds, their relationships with one-another, and our relationships with them. This neural habit becomes active as early as in two day old infants — from an evolutionary perspective, the insight it may provide has clearly been prioritised as critical to our survival.

---

## Applications for Speakers and Presenters

But what does this have to do with writing a speech or creating a presentation? The key is understanding that insights from Default-Mode thinking can be useful when crafting a message that resonates deeply with an audience.

For example, if you're drafting a presentation to deliver to software developers at an industry conference, it's important to understand what their specific motives, gripes, and aspirations might be. Insights that you gain from default-mode thinking could be the 'light-bulb' moment that helps you craft a message that will resonate and demonstrate that you have seen things from their perspective, earning trust. 

Similarly, if you're creating a presentation for a group of business managers, default-mode thinking may help shine a light on their unique perspective, pressures and concerns — in essence, it is the **optimum time** to contemplate what it's like to be in someone else's shoes.

It's also worth noting that default-mode thinking can happen at any time, but will *most likely* occur when we're **NOT** actively engaged in a task. You might have a KEY insight while walking the dog or unloading the dishwasher, and these moments can be incredibly valuable when it comes to shaping your message.

What might seem abstract or tangential at the time may take on a far greater significance once you can see it in the context of everything else and can connect the dots. So, it's important to be aware of these moments and to capture your ideas as they come to you, ready to incorporate them later on.

---

## Three Ideas to Harness Default-Mode Thinking

1. **Set aside time for reflection:** While default-mode thinking can happen at any time, it's also important to set aside dedicated time for reflection. This can be as simple as stepping away from the keyboard and going for a walk, but the key is to give yourself time to let your mind wander and daydream a little.

2. **Capture everything:** Capturing your ideas as they come to you is crucial to being able to harness insights from default-mode thinking for public speaking. Keep a small notebook or a recording device (such as your smartphone) with you at all times, so you're always ready to capture an idea when it strikes.

3. **Organisation:** Visual aids such as mind-maps, diagrams, and post-it notes can be a great way to nurture and organise insights from default-mode thinking. These aids allow you to organise your thoughts in a visual way and help you to see connections and patterns that may not be immediately obvious.

---

In summary, default-mode thinking is a powerful in-built tool that we can use to craft effective and impactful messages. By recognising the social insights delivered by this brain configuration we may better understand our audience's unique perspective and create messages that resonate with them. 

Next time you have an idea while you're doing something else, ***DON'T IGNORE IT*** — it might be the key nugget of gold necessary to craft a better speech or presentation.
