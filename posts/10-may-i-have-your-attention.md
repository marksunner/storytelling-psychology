# May I Have Your Attention?

*By Mark Sunner — Digital Ape Training*
*October 14, 2022*

---

Hang on — can it be true? Leo Tolstoy's masterpiece *Anna Karenina* opens with *exactly* the same literary device as my 2 year old grandson's favourite lift-the-flap book — *Where's Spot*, by Eric Hill?

Amazingly, the answer is yes! And once you look for it, you soon realise the same technique is used by a vast number of authors to seize the reader's attention from the outset.

Distilled to its core essence, this technique could be summarised as: **Crisis strikes a settled world.**

---

## Two Different Books, Same Opening Device

**Where's Spot:**
> "Dinnertime, Spot! Where's Spot?"

**Anna Karenina:**
> *All was confusion in the Oblonsky's House. The wife had found out that the husband was having an affair with their former French governess, and had announced to the husband that she could not live in the same house with him.*

Both stories start with an upending of the status quo. The reason this is so effective is because the human mind is endlessly fascinated by unexpected change. Whenever we encounter it, we immediately want to know: What does it mean? Is this change for the good or the bad? Unexpected change makes us curious, and curious is how we should feel in the opening of an effective story.

Where *is* Spot? Even his food is still in his bowl — he might be under a bus somewhere? And what *was* the husband up to with that French governess — if he's been caught, things could surely be about to unravel?

In either case, this is the stuff of intrigue, and in an instant we are caught in its delicious trap, desperate to find out, what happens next.

---

## What Does This Mean for Presentations?

Quite a lot. In the same way a good author aims to first grab the reader's attention, making them eager to read on — as presenters we can attempt to trigger a similar effect in our audience at the beginning of a talk.

Many presentations (including my early ones) start with an all too familiar cadence — it goes something like this:

> *Hello everyone, my name is Mark, I work for Acme Widgets Inc, and today I would like to talk to you about the latest trends in widget strategies..*

The problem here is this is the exact opposite of seizing the audience's attention. Not only is this information probably on a title slide, but it is also totally unengaging, uninteresting, and as a result telegraphs permission to mentally switch-off.

Tolstoy and Hill do not start their books with an ISBN description. Instead, they drop us straight into the action — and this approach can work equally well when starting a presentation.

---

## Examples of Engaging Openings

Obviously how you approach an intro will depend entirely on the subject at hand. But, by way of example, imagine opening a presentation with one of the following:

1. *"It was 11 o'clock on Wednesday, July 10th, 1996, sat at my desk I received an email, the content of which would change my life forever.."*

2. *"There are two hundred billion stars in the Milky Way galaxy — but, did you realise there are as many neural connections in a single cubic centimeter of brain tissue? Sitting on your shoulders is the most complicated object in the known universe — but we know surprisingly little about its inner workings. However, thanks to an accidental discovery in Italy in 1998, we now know something very revealing.."*

3. *"The first part of my talk today is intended to scare you. I want you to think very carefully about what you do in your job. Now, I want you to ask yourself the following question — could a machine do what you do? No? Are you absolutely sure about that? Allow me to introduce you to the latest advances in AI.."*

## The Psychology Behind It

From a psychological perspective, it could be argued this technique is not dissimilar to some forms of click-bait. Have you ever seen a link that reads something like "Could the International Space Station benefit from parquet flooring? The answer may shock you!" — and yes, such links are super annoying, often absurd, but be honest, has your curiosity never been piqued enough to click? The answer may shock you ;-)

This is because effective curiosity-candy releases a tiny amount of dopamine in the brain, and this is what makes us truly *want* to know more. The major difference between click-bait and an engaging opening however, is that click-bait is a clever advertisement masquerading behind a hollow promise — whereas presentations are [hopefully] substantive, informative, and will lead an audience towards a genuine outcome.

Opening a presentation in this manner is naturally more gripping, profound, emotional, and original. It is also hard to ignore because it fires up exactly the same neural circuitry employed so successfully by Tolstoy and Hill, not to mention the majority of our literary favorites.

---

Next time you open a talk or presentation give it a try — your audience's engagement and attention should benefit right from the outset.
