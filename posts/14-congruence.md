# Congruence: A Warning from History

*By Mark Sunner — Digital Ape Training*
*October 1, 2022*

---

On September 26, 1960, history was made when Vice President Richard Nixon and Senator John F. Kennedy participated in the first-ever nationally televised presidential debate. This landmark event was broadcast live on both television and radio, and it would go on to shape the modern political landscape in ways that no one could have predicted.

When the debate was over, people who had listened to it on the radio thought that Nixon had won. But those who had watched it on television were convinced that Kennedy was the clear winner. What could possibly explain such a polarised difference of opinion?

---

## The Power of Visual Presence

The answer can be found by looking at the footage of the debate. While Nixon may have sounded good, he was completely outgunned in terms of his physical presence. Nixon had refused to rehearse and also declined stage makeup, and as a result, he looked sweaty and uncomfortable under the harsh studio lights. Kennedy, on the other hand, had rehearsed extensively and appeared confident and self-assured. He also demonstrated an awareness of the new medium of television by hiring a team of stylists who applied stage makeup and ensured that his appearance was perfectly manicured for the event.

Before the debate, Nixon was the clear favourite, with a 6% polling advantage. But after the debate, polls suddenly showed Kennedy had taken the lead. When the Nixon team heard the damning poll results, they scrambled to be better rehearsed for the remaining debates. However, even vice presidents do not get a second chance at a first impression, and in the court of public opinion, the die had already been cast.

---

## The Lesson Learned

Two decades later, conservatives appeared to have learned an important lesson. Then-candidate Ronald Reagan, an experienced actor, delivered a well-rehearsed, telegenic performance that contrasted sharply with the stilted appearance of incumbent Jimmy Carter.

In both of these examples, nonverbal communication played a crucial role in shaping the outcome of the debates. The authenticity of nonverbal communication is always believed over what is being said, and a congruent performance that confirms a presenter's conviction can move an audience in a way that words alone cannot.

---

## Why This Still Matters

This lesson is just as relevant today as it was 60 years ago. As a public speaker, it is essential to be conscious of both the words you are saying and the nonverbal cues you are sending. Your appearance, body language, and overall presence can all contribute to the effectiveness of your message and your ability to connect with your audience. 

As French psychologist Max Dessoir so eloquently put it, **"only the convinced are convincing."** If you truly believe in what you are saying and present it with confidence, your audience is much more likely to be convinced as well.
