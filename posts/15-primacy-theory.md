# Primacy Theory

*By Mark Sunner — Digital Ape Training*
*November 3, 2019*

---

> *"Have you ever noticed how some supermarkets play psychological tricks? As soon as you go in, you're hit with fresh fruit and veg. And you go in thinking, this is a fresh shop! Everything here is fresh! I will do well here.."*
>
> *— Eddie Izzard, The Definite Article*

I'd love to tell you that I figured out an amazing secret, buried in a spreadsheet, which lead me towards an epiphany. Truth is, it happened by accident.

Many moons ago I taught a number of IT courses that ranged from one to five days in duration. But, over a period of time I realised something was not quite right with the 1 day course. At the end of all courses we would circulate evaluation forms. I had noticed that the overall mark for the 1 day course was always a tiny bit lower than for courses that were two or more days in length - weird? It was not just me either - this anomaly persisted no matter who taught the course, and it had developed something of a reputation for being a Bermuda triangle when it came to feedback.

Now, I can probably guess what you are thinking - all things being equal, the course simply needed improvement? But, you are going to have to trust me when I tell you that this course was already brilliant - an exquisitely manicured 8hr bonsai tree of learning, jam packed with stellar content, great structure, the perfect balance of lecture vs practical etc. The course was not the problem - there had to be some other reason, and finding it was driving me crazy.

I decided to put several years' worth of evaluation data into a spreadsheet - we were constantly updating material to keep pace with technology, so I wanted to see if the problem had always been there. Once graphed, it was more noticeable - and was indeed consistent. And yet, I was no closer to figuring out why?

The training company I worked for had over 60 courses on its books, including several other 1 day courses that I did not teach. Did the same phenomenon exist on the other 1 day courses too? I popped everything into a spreadsheet and corollated the lot. BINGO - the same quirk **did** exist across multiple 1 day courses! Now I felt like I was on to something.

As with my course, the difference was slight, not enough to be flagged as a problem, however it was there if you went looking. But, there was something else too - poking through the data was one lone-wolf bucking the trend. Richard, from the Languages department. Richard, teaching '1 day C Primer' was consistently scoring a higher mark than everyone else? I felt like I'd discovered a hidden planet via its gravitational footprint - now I just needed to train a telescope on its location. Come on Watson - the game's afoot! I booked myself onto one of Richard's courses determined to reverse engineer his alchemy.

---

Richard was an amazing instructor. He played in a band and it was evident that showmanship was in his DNA. Infectious enthusiasm, wonderful explanations, a relaxed cheeky style - it was easy to see why the students loved him. Just one problem - I was not having the epiphany I thought I would. To the best of my ability, I too tried to blend being at the top of my game technically with genuine passion/enthusiasm - if I was brutally honest, I could not see *that* much of a difference? Whatever pixie-dust Richard was sprinkling it was subtle & elusive. At the end of Richard's course I scanned his evaluations. True to form, a perfect score - I knew it would be - I'd been in the room and the atmosphere had been electric. The students had learned loads and really enjoyed themselves - somewhere at the back of my mind I knew that the atmosphere Richard had engendered was just better somehow - goddammit - what witchcraft was this?!

In the meantime the delivery of my 1 day course was rolling around bi-weekly and I was no closer to figuring out why it was giving people a banana mouth. One observation struck - I recognised the palpable atmosphere in Richard's class. It felt much more like that of a 3 or 4 day course - when enough time had passed so everyone really gets comfortable and able to properly immerse themselves - somehow Richard had got to this point in record time - but how?

Frustrated with my inability to decode Richard's magic, I let my guard slip one morning and shot from the hip. I opened a 1 day course with the following:

> *"Morning everyone, before we start I'd just like to say one thing. We only have 1 day to cover a lot of ground. If this were a 3 or a 4 day course, one thing that would naturally happen is you would really get to know me, I would get to know you, and all the personal barriers would come crashing down ..when that happens everyone feels a lot freer about asking questions - so.. could we all just pretend we did that bit?"*

To my great surprise everybody laughed. Eh? This was not meant to be clever or funny? These were my unvarnished thoughts that had accidentally slipped out - why was everyone laughing? Still, there was no denying it felt good, and now even I was laughing - I'd never made a class laugh within 60 seconds before, and found I was staring at a room full of smiles. I went on to deliver the course as normal, but something was different - there it was - that 'atmosphere' the slow thaw of 12 strangers had been replaced with an instant warmth - reader: it felt amazing!

I finished the course but almost everyone stuck around - we had all bonded more than normal - but I did not know why? Eventually the last person left and I gathered up the evaluations. With some trepidation I slowly read the feedback - WOW - not only a better score - but lots of highly valuable and very positive comments.

---

This was fascinating. I'd taught this course well over a hundred times and knew it beat perfect - this meant I knew for a fact that the ONLY thing different was my accidental remark at the beginning. I thought back to Richard's course again. He had indeed disarmed the audience with his first remark - but it was so fleeting that I had not realised it was significant. My mind was racing - what did this mean? Did the first sixty seconds really have the power to change the dynamic of the entire day - perhaps it was a fluke? I needed to know if it could be repeated, and I'd get my chance soon enough.

Two weeks later, in a different room, with 12 different people, I delivered the same opening gambit word-for-word, the only difference was this time it was deliberate. Again heartfelt laughter, again 12 smiles, all within 60 seconds - so far so good. The course went note-for-note as the previous run had - right down to everyone sticking around for over an hour after the course had finished - always a good sign. Eventually I read through the evaluations and was delighted to get confirmation the course had indeed gone down a storm - and yet, the only difference was that opening. Five courses later I knew for sure that this one tiny change really was making a huge difference. But how? I simply had to figure this out.

Around this time (approximately 25 years ago) Comedian in a dress, with fabulous heels, **Eddie Izzard**, was highly en vogue and the airwaves were filled with snippets of his clever routines delighting anyone with a functioning cerebellum.

One routine in particular struck a chord - supermarkets. Specifically, that many store entrances had morphed into a garden of eden designed to make shoppers attach a 'fresh produce' label to the whole store experience - yeah, it was definitely funnier when he said it. But as with any good comedy routine, it held more than a grain of truth, and was in-fact hinting at a whole body of psychology called **Primacy theory**. The observation caused the **tangled-investigation-board of my dyslexic mind** to stick a pin in Eddie's supermarket routine and connect it to presenting.

If the first 60 seconds of a presentation was the entrance to a metaphorical supermarket - had my previous intro been welcoming new customers into a shopping isle full of loo brushes and bleach? Hmmm, perhaps not that bad, but it certainly might have been the crisp and biscuit isle - yikes! Had I been setting up my students for the best possible training experience right from the first second they encountered me - perhaps not - slowly the dots were starting to connect.

---

In trying to figure out what was happening, I eventually came to realise that the human brain is optimised for our ice-age ancestors, for whom resources were **extremely** scarce. What is perhaps less obvious, is that 'Thinking' burns calories. A lot of calories. A grand master chess player can burn a staggering 6000 calories a day without even leaving the chessboard. Millennia of human evolution has selected a predisposition for 'snap judgements'. We have evolved these unconscious split second judgements partly as a means to conserve resources and partly to avoid being paralysed by indecision when facing danger. Snap judgements saved our ancestors lives. This is a highly efficient way of operating, but there is a downside. Snap judgements act as a filter - a bias lens through-which all subsequent information is processed. We not only judge a book by its cover, but we become invested in proving to ourselves that we were right.

For a masterclass on this subject and why it matters, take a look at [this TEDx talk](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rk_SMBIW1mg) by Presentation and body language ninja [Mark Bowden](https://www.linkedin.com/in/markbowden1/). Mark's talk explains the situation brilliantly. But, it is perhaps his opening gambit that I love the most:

> *"So, you've already decided whether you like me or not.."*

These first 10 words grab the audience from the outset, shortly before hurtling them around a barnstorming rollercoaster into their own psyche - it's genius - and if you have not seen it already, you are in for a treat.

---

So what does all this mean? The lesson I learned from my early training journey is that it's worth focusing a disproportionate amount of effort crafting the first sixty seconds of any presentation or talk. A good strong opening needs to let the audience know you are really invested in *them* and they are safe to trust *you* with the precious gift of their full attention.

Going back to the training / supermarket analogy. I realised that longer courses were more like French hypermarkets - the entrance experience could afford to be full of power tools, lawn mowers and crazy stuff if the rest of the store was big enough that a customers cerebral shopping trolley could still pick up enough interesting items to redress the balance. But the smaller the store, the more that *initial entrance* really mattered in terms of shaping the customer experience.

These days I tailor the opening gambit of every talk, regardless of whether it's 5 minutes or 5 days. It's really important that I make it highly personal to the audience. My goal is always the same - let the audience know that I am fully present with them, and whatever I am about to say, is just for *them*.

I want the audience to know that it's a fresh presentation! All the material is fresh! They will do well here..
