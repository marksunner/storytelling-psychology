# Perfecting Imperfection

*How the Pratfall Effect & Benign Violation Theory might elevate your stage presence*

*By Mark Sunner — Digital Ape Training*

---

Many of us will have encountered moments where we've made a mistake or blundered in front of an audience. Whether it's getting tongue-twisted, tripping on your way to the podium, or forgetting an important point in a presentation, these things happen to the best of us — and if it's not happened yet, trust me... it's just a matter of time!

But, while these mistakes may feel a tad embarrassing in the moment, they can actually have a surprisingly positive effect on our audience's perception. This well documented phenomenon is known as the **"Pratfall Effect"**, and it suggests that making mistakes or appearing imperfect can actually increase our likability to others.

## One Important Caveat

However, there is one important caveat — and it's a doozy. This phenomenon will only work in your favour if you have locked-in a good first impression, and set the right tone. Unfortunately, failure to cement a good first impression means any subsequent pratfall violations will likely be perceived negatively.

## The Psychology Behind It

But what is the psychological reasoning behind the pratfall effect? Enter another academically supported term called **"Benign Violation Theory"**, which posits that people are more likely to find something funny or enjoyable if it violates their expectations in a benign or non-threatening way. 

This theory helps to explain the pratfall effect, as making a mistake or blunder can violate the audience's expectations and create a sense of surprise or amusement. This can increase the speaker's warmth and likability, as it shows their humanity and vulnerability, and can reveal a more relatable and genuine layer to their personality.

## Practical Tips

So, how can you embrace the pratfall effect and benign violation theory to enhance your public speaking skills? Here are a few practical tips to consider:

- **Don't be afraid to use self-deprecating humour:** Admitting to your own mistakes or imperfections can help to build a stronger connection with your audience, as it shows that you are human and relatable.

- **Use humour to challenge the audience's expectations:** Telling a joke or using a punchline that plays with the audience's expectations can create a sense of surprise and amusement, which can make your speech more engaging and memorable.

- **Embrace your mistakes:** If you do happen to make a blunder during your presentation, don't panic! Acknowledge it, laugh it off, and move on. This can show your audience that you are comfortable with your imperfections, which can increase their likability towards you.

---

In conclusion, the pratfall effect and benign violation theory are useful tools for public speaking, as they allow you to show your humanity and vulnerability in a way that can engage and connect with your audience.

By incorporating these principles into your presentations, you can use humour and self-deprecation to create a sense of surprise and amusement, which can make your message more memorable and impactful. 

So the next time you trip over a cable, completely lose your train of thought, or accidentally take the radio-mic for a trip to the loo (yup — I've done all three)... don't panic! Your pillow-biting mishap might just be the key to actually elevating your connection with your audience.
