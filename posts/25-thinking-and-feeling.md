# Thinking AND Feeling: The Role of Emotion when Presenting

*By Mark Sunner — Digital Ape Training*

---

In order to be effective public speakers, it is important to understand the way in which the human brain processes information. The neocortex, or the "WHAT" level, is responsible for rational and analytical thought. However, the higher-priority and more calorie efficient part of the brain that controls behaviour, the limbic brain, is far more governed by emotion. This disconnection between the inner-monologue of the neocortex and the emotions being felt by the limbic system explain why we sometimes have difficulty expressing our feelings in words.

However, it can be exceptionally potent when speakers trigger an audience to both ***THINK*** and ***FEEL*** something. This is because the limbic brain, which controls our emotions and decision-making, plays a crucial role in how we process information and make decisions. Emotions serve an evolutionary purpose as they allow us to make super-fast judgments, without the calorie burning inefficiency of more deep/considered thinking, all based on our life experiences and programming. When we communicate from the "inside out," or speak directly to the limbic brain, we are able to tap into these speedily processed emotional responses, which can make a more impactful, and permanent connection with the audience.

To create more effective messaging, try connecting with the audience on an emotional level. This can be done through storytelling, figurative language, and how you say it. By speaking to both the neocortex AND the limbic brain, we can effectively land a far more holistic message.

---

## Three Ideas to Add More Emotional Wallop to Your Delivery

**1. Use storytelling to make your audience feel something**

Don't just tell them what happened, show them. Paint a picture or describe the arc of an event and put them in the room, tell them exactly what it felt like to be there, and don't be afraid to actually show some emotion and genuine feelings to really pull at heartstrings.

**2. Use specific language that's going to make your audience feel something**

Analogies and Metaphors are a great way to make your message more memorable. But, take time to carefully select the exact descriptive/expressive words you are going to use, and crucially, practice how you are going to deliver them — what initially works in your head will likely sound very different when first voiced for real.

**3. Vary the pace and tone**

A consistent cadence will put people to sleep, so it's important to constantly mix things up. For example, use a slower more contemplative pace to convey a sense of solemnity, but switch to a faster more energetic pace to convey excitement or enthusiasm. In addition, rhetorical devices such as anaphora (repetition — a Boris Johnson favourite) or loaded questions to make it more of a conversation, will help engage the audience on an emotional level.

---

## Conclusion

In conclusion, understanding the way in which the brain processes information can be crucial for crafting effective messaging. By focusing on both "thinking" and "feeling" elements in our communication, we can more fully connect with the audience. The bottom line is that by speaking to both the neocortex and the limbic brain our messages are more impactful, and therefore more likely to be remembered long after the speech or presentation has finished.
