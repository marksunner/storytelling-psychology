---

It's common for people to try to hide or downplay their nerves before speaking in front of an audience, especially if they are in a leadership or public-facing role. When we are faced with the prospect of speaking in public, our brains release stress hormones, which can affect our performance and confidence. This is due to the complex chemical responses that occur in the brain when we feel threatened or vulnerable.

While it's natural to feel nervous, it's important to recognise that this fear is a common and normal part of the human experience. So the truth is, whilst not everyone may admit it, **we ALL experience some level of anxiety or nervousness when presenting in front of a group** — it is a hardwired response.

But why should the act of speaking in front of an audience trigger such a cascade of unhelpful brain chemistry? By understanding the evolutionary reasons behind this fear and learning to manage it, we can become far more confident and effective public speakers.

---

## The Evolutionary Roots

From an evolutionary perspective, it is likely that this fear has roots in our primitive ancestors' experiences. In small hunter-gatherer communities, being able to effectively communicate and persuade others was essential for survival. Those who were able to effectively convey their ideas and thoughts were more likely to be successful in securing resources, forming alliances, and avoiding danger.

However, speaking in front of a large group also carried risks. In these societies, being ostracised or rejected by the group could have serious consequences, including isolation and vulnerability to predators. It is possible that the fear response that many people experience when public speaking is a remnant of this primal fear of rejection or social isolation.

---

## The Fight or Flight Response

The fight or flight response, a physiological response to perceived danger, may also play a role in public speaking anxiety. When we feel threatened or vulnerable, our bodies release adrenaline and cortisol, which can cause physical symptoms such as a racing heart, sweaty palms, and shaking. While this response can be helpful in dangerous situations, it can also be triggered by non-life threatening events such as giving a talk or delivering a presentation.

While the fear of public speaking may have had evolutionary roots, it is important to remember that in modern society, the stakes are generally much lower. While speaking to an audience may still carry some social risks, the consequences are rarely as severe as they were for our ancestors. By understanding the evolutionary reasons behind this fear and learning to manage it, individuals can become far more confident and effective public speakers.

---

## Five Things to Help Manage Nerves

**1. Always practice:** The more you practice, the more comfortable and confident you will become with your material. This can include rehearsing in front of a mirror, recording yourself and viewing the playback or perhaps the most ideal, practicing in front of a small group of trusted friends or colleagues. Practising in front of friends, will still trigger the hormone response, so this has the added benefit of allowing you to get comfortable operating in this altered mental state ahead of time.

**2. Know your audience:** Understanding the demographics and interests of your audience can help you tailor your message and make a stronger connection with them.

**3. Use positive self-talk:** It's natural to have negative thoughts before speaking in front of a large group, but try to reframe them into positive affirmations. For example, instead of thinking "I'm going to mess up," try thinking "I am prepared and capable of delivering a great presentation."

**4. Take deep breaths:** Deep breathing can help calm your nerves and improve your focus. Take a few deep breaths before you begin speaking and try to maintain a slow and steady pace while speaking.

**5. Visualise success:** Close your eyes and imagine yourself delivering a successful and well-received presentation. Whilst this might sound simple, positive visualisation has been proven to help boost confidence and reduce anxiety, for this reasons it is now an accepted component of elite sports psychology.

---

Remember, everyone gets nervous before speaking in front of a large audience. It's normal and natural. By practicing and using these techniques, you can learn to manage your nerves and become a more confident and effective public speaker.
