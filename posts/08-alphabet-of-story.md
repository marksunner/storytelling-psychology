## The Basic Building Blocks of Storytelling

### Concept and Theme

The backbone of any narrative lies in its concept—the unique angle or lens that sets your story apart—and its theme—the central message around which your story revolves. Identifying these elements before anything else will act as a compass, and help steer your storytelling throughout the rest of the process.

### Structure: Hook, Build, Pay-off

A captivating narrative typically follows a three-act structure: a **Hook** that piques interest, a **Build** that deepens engagement, and a **Pay-off** that delivers a satisfying resolution.

### Characters: Protagonist and Antagonist

The roles of 'heroes' and 'villains' are pivotal in storytelling. Your 'hero' could be a company, a product, or an idea, while the 'villain' might be a problem your product solves or a challenge your idea overcomes.

### Inciting Incident and Climax

The inciting incident propels your narrative into action, while the climax brings it to a head, where the 'hero' confronts the 'villain'. These elements keep your audience engaged and invested.

Why are these elements considered the 'alphabet'? Just as letters form the basis for all words and sentences, these elements are the fundamental units that build every narrative, regardless of its form or genre. They are the universal touchstones that allow a story to connect, engage, and resonate with its audience.

## A Practical Example: Implementing the Storytelling Alphabet in a SaaS Company

MANY moons ago (circa 1999), I worked at an internet startup, **MessageLabs**, a then fledgling internet-based security company that was created long before "cloud" was an internet-verb or "SaaS" had been coined as an acronym. The pioneering nature of our solution was also its greatest challenge — how do you pitch something entirely new/unknown against a long established incumbent? We used the storytelling alphabet to create a narrative that could displace long-held and entrenched views and effectively plant a seed for a new way of thinking.

The unique concept of our narrative was our product—an internet level security solution that leveraged superior computational power to filter malicious content from customers' email and web traffic. Our theme was secure internet usage, equated with the simplicity of drinking clean water from a tap. We don't boil water before drinking it; similarly, we shouldn't be expected to deal with harmful internet traffic at the individual level. This theme, like a map, provides the broader context, helping us navigate every twist and turn in the narrative.

We used the three-act structure to present our narrative. We introduced the problem—internet toxicity and inadequate solutions—in the Hook. In the Build, we presented our unique solution—a cloud-based service that leverages advanced threat detection at the source. The Pay-off was demonstrating how our product significantly improved the user's internet experience.

Our 'hero' was our cloud-based position, and the 'villain' was the evolving landscape of internet threats that conventional antivirus scanners struggle to combat effectively.

The inciting incident was recognising the insufficiency of traditional internet security measures, while the climax was the moment our 'hero' showcased its superior protection capabilities.

Once we knew the answers to each of these fundamental building blocks, they could be used to adapt a limitless supply of presentations, speeches and marketing collateral — and this narrative backbone ran through the entire company like Brighton through a stick of rock, serving as a guiding light and powerful motivating force.

## Conclusion

Just as the letters of the alphabet form the foundation of written language, or phrases underpin pop music, a 'storytelling alphabet' can serve as a versatile tool for creativity and communication. My time at MessageLabs was a seminal lesson in how understanding these foundational elements can help us craft narratives that engage, inform, and perhaps even transform our audience's prior entrenched beliefs.

Beyond its practical use, the storytelling alphabet can also act as a lens for deeper understanding by revealing the hidden architecture of impactful stories, be they TED talks, business pitches, or blog posts. But primarily, the formula equips us with a time-honoured method to bypass calcified biases and open up fresh perspectives. It delivers an imaginative way to see and engage with the world, with the potential to transform not only our individual narratives but even our collective worldview.
