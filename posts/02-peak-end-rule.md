# The Peak-End Rule

*By Mark Sunner — Digital Ape Training*

---

Have you ever left a presentation feeling like you barely remember what was said, even though you were paying attention the whole time? If so, you're not alone. It's a common experience, and it turns out there's a scientific reason behind it.

It's called the **Peak-End Rule**, and it's a principle that suggests that people tend to remember the emotional peak and the ending of an experience more vividly than anything else. So, in a presentation, it's super important to focus on delivering a strong peak and a good ending if you want to leave a lasting impression.

But how do you do that? Well, one way is to focus the core message of your presentation in the peak. This allows your audience to take away the most important part of your talk, and helps to ensure that it will be remembered long after the presentation is over.

## "But What About All My Research?"

Now, I know what you might be thinking — "But what about all of the research and facts that I've spent hours putting together? Does this mean my audience will forget all those valuable details?" 

The short answer is yes, your audience probably won't remember every single fact and figure. But that doesn't mean that the research isn't important.

In fact, the purpose of the research is to create trust and establish a solid foundation for your main message. For example, let's say you're giving a presentation about the effectiveness of storytelling as a format. By including detailed research that supports your message, you're establishing credibility and reassuring your audience that your message is based on solid evidence. This helps to create the necessary trust for your audience to accept your main message, which in this case might be something like "Storytelling works and is backed up by neuroscience."

## Engage Emotions, Nail the Ending

But here's the thing: even though the research is necessary to establish trust and credibility, it's the emotional **PEAK** and the **ENDING** of your presentation that will be remembered the most vividly by your audience. 

So, in order to deliver a strong peak and a good ending, you need to engage your audience's emotions and conclude your presentation in a way that leaves a lasting impact. This can be achieved through the use of vivid descriptions, storytelling, or other techniques that engage your audience emotionally.

So, the next time you're giving a presentation, remember the Peak-End Rule and focus on delivering a strong peak and a good ending. Your audience will have a better experience, and crucially, will remember your core message far longer than just a few minutes after your talk or presentation has finished.
